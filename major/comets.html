Comets are frozen leftovers from the formation of the solar system composed of dust, rock, and ices. They range from a few miles to tens of miles wide, but as they orbit closer to the Sun, they heat up and spew gases and dust into a glowing head that can be larger than a planet. This material forms a tail that stretches millions of miles.

The Kuiper Belt is a doughnut-shaped region of icy objects beyond the orbit of Neptune. It is home to Pluto and most of the known dwarf planets. There are likely billions of comets orbiting our Sun in the Kuiper Belt and even more distant Oort Cloud.

Edmond Halley

Born: Nov. 8, 1656, Haggerston, England

Died: Jan. 14, 1742, Greenwich, England

An English astronomer and mathematician who was the first to calculate the orbit of a comet later named after him. He is also noted for his role in the publication of Isaac Newtonâ€™s Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica.

Halley published in 1705 A Synopsis of the Astronomy of Comets, in which he described the parabolic orbits of 24 comets that had been observed from 1337 to 1698. In 1718, he found that Sirius and Arcturus had slightly shifted their positions with respect to their neighbours, discovering what modern astronomers call proper motion.

Caroline Herschel

Born: March 16, 1750, Hannover, Germany

Died: January 9, 1848, Hannover, Germany

A German-born British astronomer who was a pioneer in the field and is considered the first professional female astronomer. She made important contributions to the work of her brother Sir William Herschel, executing many of the calculations connected with his studies.

In 1786 she became the first woman to discover a comet. She also completed the cataloging of 2,500 nebulae and many star clusters. Learn more about her brother on the nebulae page.

Johann Franz Encke

Born: Sept. 23, 1791, Hamburg, Germany

Died: Aug. 26, 1865, Spandau, Germany

A German astronomer who in 1819 established the period of the comet now known as Encke's Comet. Encke's comet was the second periodic comet discovered (Halley's Comet was the first). He is also known for his discovery of Encke's Gap, a gap in the outer part of Saturn's outermost ring.

He worked under the direction of Carl Friedrich Gauss for a period of time. Learn more about Gauss on the asteroids page.
