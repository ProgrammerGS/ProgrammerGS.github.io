Asteroids

Back To Top

Asteroids, sometimes called minor planets, are rocky, airless remnants left over from the early formation of our solar system about 4.6 billion years ago. Most asteroids can be found orbiting the Sun between Mars and Jupiter within the main asteroid belt. Asteroids range in size from Vesta – the largest at about 329 miles (530 kilometers) in diameter – to bodies that are less than 33 feet (10 meters) across. The total mass of all the asteroids combined is less than that of Earth's Moon.

New asteroids are discovered quite frequently. When a new asteroid is discovered nowadays, its discoverer gets to choose its name (the rules surrounding naming asteroids is not very strict). There are asteroids named after famous people or places. A fairly up-to-date list of asteroid names can be found here.

Giuseppe Piazzi

Born: July 16, 1746, Ponte di Valtellina, Italy

Died: July 22, 1826, Naples, Italy

An Italian astronomer who discovered and named the first asteroid, or “minor planet,” Ceres.

He produced a great catalog of the positions of 7,646 stars and demonstrated that most stars are in motion relative to the Sun. He also founded the Observatory of Palermo and discovered the high proper motion of the important double star 61 Cygni there.

Carl Friedrich Gauss

Born: April 30, 1777, Brunswick, Germany

Died: February 23, 1855, Göttingen, Germany

A German mathematician, generally regarded as one of the greatest mathematicians of all time for his contributions to number theory, geometry, probability theory, geodesy, planetary astronomy, the theory of functions, and potential theory.

He rediscovered the asteroid Ceres discovered by Giuseppe Piazzi after it vanished behind the sun before enough observations could be taken to calculate its orbit with sufficient accuracy to know where it would reappear.

Max Wolf

Born: June 21, 1863, Heidelberg, Germany

Died: Oct. 3, 1932, Heidelberg, Germany

A German astronomer who applied photography to the search for asteroids and discovered 228 of them. He was the first to use the stereocomparator (a type of stereoscopic viewer), which greatly helps in the discovery and identification of variable or moving objects in celestial photographs.

Through his photographic studies he established the presence of dark clouds of interstellar matter in the Milky Way Galaxy. In 1906 he discovered Achilles, the first Trojan asteroid, asteroids inhabiting any planet’s stable Lagrange points.
