Nebula, any of the various tenuous clouds of gas and dust that occur in interstellar space. The term was formerly applied to any object outside the solar system that had a diffuse appearance rather than a pointlike image, which unfortunately includes two unrelated classes of objects:

  • Galaxies, which are enormous collections of stars and gas
  • Galactic nebulae, which are composed of the interstellar medium (the gas between the stars, with its accompanying small solid particles) within a single galaxy

Today the term nebula generally refers exclusively to the latter, but in this page, we will be focusing on the former. Variable stars, defined below, are also a big part of this page.

Variable stars are any stars whose observed light varies notably in intensity. The changes in brightness may be periodic, semiregular, or completely irregular. The Cepheids and RR Lyrae stars are typical examples of such variables.

Charles Messier

Born: June 26, 1730, Badonviller, France

Died: April 12, 1817, Paris, France

A French astronomer who was the first to compile a systematic catalog of nebulae and star clusters. Many of these nebulae, including some of the most prominent, are still known by his catalog numbers.

He was also the first in France to observe the anticipated return of Halley’s Comet in 1758–59. Click here to learn more about Halley's comet.

William Herschel

Born: November 15, 1738, Hannover, Germany

Died: August 25, 1822, Slough, England

A German-born British astronomer, the founder of sidereal astronomy for the systematic observation of the stars and nebulae beyond the solar system.

He discovered the planet Uranus, hypothesized that nebulae are composed of stars, and developed a theory of stellar evolution. He made his own telescopes that proved far superior even to those used at the Greenwich Observatory at the time. He was also knighted in 1816. Learn more about his sister on the comets page.

Edwin Hubble

Born: November 20, 1889, Marshfield, MO, U.S.

Died: September 28, 1953, San Marino, CA, U.S.

An American astronomer who is generally regarded as the leading observational cosmologist of the 20th century.

In 1923 Hubble found Cepheid variable stars in the Andromeda Nebula. The fluctuations in light of these stars enabled Hubble to determine the nebula’s distance using the relationship between the period of the Cepheid fluctuations and its luminosity.

The famous Hubble Space Telescope launched into space in 1990 and still being used today is named after him.
